July is for Independence

Nobody ever felt independent because of a great spreadsheet. But independence isn't the absence of obligation. It's having built something sturdy enough to stand on its own through a slow quarter, a job change, a surprise bill.

Not everyone reading this runs a business, and that's the point. For an owner, independence is the day the company runs without you in the room. For a family, it's a reserve that turns a crisis into an inconvenience and a retirement that arrives on your terms. Same game, different scoreboards.

July is the exact midpoint of the year, the most honest month to look up, read the numbers without flinching, and adjust while there's still runway. The people who finish strong usually aren't the ones who scrambled hardest in December. They're the ones who checked in now.

TikTok Tax Tips

Trending or Trouble?

Each month we put a TikTok tax tip on trial. And our jury has actually read the tax code.

This month: "Buy a G-Wagon and write it off."

Half true, which is what makes it dangerous. A vehicle over 6,000 lbs does skip the strict luxury-auto depreciation caps, and Section 179 plus bonus depreciation can expense a big chunk of it in year one. The part that TikTok creators skip: the write-off only counts to the extent you use it for business (and you'd better have the mileage log, because it's one of the most audited lines there is). It generally has to be bought, not leased. And when you sell, that deduction comes roaring back as taxable income.

Verdict: Depends. Rarely the way it's sold by viral content creators. In short, buy what the business genuinely needs, document it, and time it on purpose. A $150K SUV bought to "beat taxes" is just a car you wanted, wearing a costume.

1913

Did You Know?

When the federal income tax began in 1913, the top rate was just 7%, and it only hit income over $500,000, about $15M today. Most Americans owed nothing, and the entire law fit in a few dozen pages. A century later, the code and regs run into the tens of thousands. The simple things never quite stay simple.

Worth Your Attention

Interest Earned

Five things that earned our interest this month. Not advice, just what caught our attention.

📖 Reading: Sam Walton: Made in America. The richest man in the country flew his own plane over rivals' lots to count cars and drove a beat-up pickup for decades. The whole book is a case for winning big by relentlessly sweating the small stuff.
🎧 Listening: Acquired on Costco. Three genuinely fun hours on how concrete floors and a $1.50 hot dog (same price since 1985) built one of the most loyal customer bases on earth. A masterclass in not squeezing the people who trust you.
🤖 Tech: Google's NotebookLM has a feature that feels like a magic trick. Upload anything: a contract, a 40-page HOA packet, your kid's school handbook. One click and it generates a surprisingly realistic podcast, with two AI hosts who actually discuss your document, banter and all, in about the time it takes to make coffee. It's free with a Google account. Very strange the first time. Genuinely useful after that. (We may have fed it this very newsletter to hear what they'd say about us.)
🔥 Trying: 3-2-1 ribs, the ideal summer weekend project. 225°F: 3 hours of smoke, 2 wrapped in foil with brown sugar and butter, 1 brushing on sauce. The hard part isn't the method. It's resisting the urge to peek. (We're accountants. We can confirm patience compounds.)
💬 Line worth keeping: "The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the waiting." (Charlie Munger)
